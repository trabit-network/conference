About

The Translational Brain Imaging Training Network (TRABIT) is an EU-funded consortium focused on developing new computational methods to analyze the "wild" type of imaging data arising in the standard clinical treatment of brain disorders.

To mark the nearing end of the project, we are organizing a virtual conference with leading keynote speakers and round table discussions on the four scientific work packages of TRABIT:

• Fetal brain disorders (with Dr. Ellen Grant, Children's Hospital Boston), Tue Sep 7th

• Brain tumors (with Dr. Ron Kikinis, Harvard Medical School, and Dr. Christos Davatzikos, University of Pennsylvania), Wed Sep 8th

• Multiple Sclerosis (with Dr. Daniel Reich, National Institutes of Health), Thu Sep 9th

• Neurovascular diseases (with Dr. Jan Kirschke, Technical University of Munich), Fri Sep 10th

You are cordially invited to attend -- the detailed schedule is below. Registration is free but mandatory.

Speakers

  • Dr. Jan Kirschke

    PD. Dr. Kirschke conducts research in quantitative neuro-oncologic and musculoskeletal imaging. Starting in 2001 with research on high resolution bone imaging, he is now head of the quantitative imaging research group at the Department of Diagnostic and Interventional Neuroradiology at TUM. He develops imaging protocols and algorithms to analyze recurrence and tumor growth in glioma patients as well as to predict osteoporotic fractures and treatment success in back pain patients. PD Dr. Kirschke studied medicine in Regensburg and Munich, where he finished his thesis in 2004. He subsequently moved to San Francisco where he worked as a postdoctoral researcher in the musculoskeletal quantitative imaging group at UCSF. In 2006 he started his training in radiology at the Department of Diagnostic and Interventional Radiology of the Technical University Munich, Germany. He was board-certified in 2012 and works as senior consultant at the Department of Diagnostic and Interventional Neuroradiology since 2013.

  • Dr. Daniel Reich

    Dr. Reich studied math and physics at Yale and earned his MD from Cornell and his PhD in neurophysiology from The Rockefeller University, where he studied how nerve cells in the visual system encode what we see. His further training includes a fellowship in diagnostic neuroradiology and residencies in radiology and neurology at Johns Hopkins Hospital. Dr. Daniel Reich is Senior Investigator at NIH/NINDS, where he directs the Translational Neuroradiology Section and leads clinical studies focusing mainly on multiple sclerosis (MS). He is also an attending neuroradiologist at the NIH Clinical Center and an Adjunct Professor of Radiology, Neurology, and Biostatistics at Johns Hopkins University. Research in Dr. Reich’s lab develops advanced MRI techniques to understand MS and adapts those techniques for clinical trials and patient care. The lab harnesses noninvasive imaging modalities to dissect biological mechanisms of tissue damage, both by performing longitudinal studies on time scales relevant for disease processes and by examining radiological-pathological correlations in autopsy tissue and animal models. Dr. Reich has authored more than 240 peer-reviewed publications, presented more than 150 invited lectures across the world, and has been principal or associate investigator of more than 50 clinical protocols. In 2018, Dr. Reich’s lab reported that human and nonhuman primates have a lymphatic system in the membranes covering the brain and showed how that system can be imaged noninvasively with MRI.

  • Dr. Ellen Grant

    Dr. Grant holds a Master of Science degree in physics and an MD from the University of Toronto. She did her radiology residency at Vancouver General Hospital in British Columbia, Canada, and her fellowship in adult and pediatric neuroradiology at the University of California, San Francisco. She is now a Professor of Radiology and Pediatrics at the Harvard Medical School. Dr. Grant headed the Division of Pediatric Radiology at Massachusetts General Hospital for five years before moving to Children's Hospital Boston to become the founding director of the Fetal-Neonatal Neuroimaging and Developmental Science Center and the first incumbent of Children's Hospital Boston Chair in Neonatology. At Children's she holds appointments in the Division of Newborn Medicine and the Department of Radiology. Dr. Grant is a co-author of two popular textbooks for clinical neuroradiology and has won a number of awards for her research efforts as well as recognition for her clinical excellence.

  • Dr. Ron Kikinis

    Dr. Ron Kikinis received his M.D. degree from the University of Zurich, Switzerland, in 1982. He trained as a resident in radiology at the University Hospital in Zurich, and as a researcher in computer vision at the ETH in Zurich, Switzerland. In 1988, he moved to Brigham & Women's Hospital, and in 1990, founded the Surgical Planning Laboratory, Department of Radiology, Brigham and Women's Hospital, Harvard Medical School, Boston, MA. In 2004, he was appointed Professor of Radiology at Harvard Medical School, and in 2010, was appointed the Robert Greenes Distinguished Director of Biomedical Informatics, Department of Radiology, Brigham and Women's Hospital. From January 2014 through February 2020, he took on a part-time position in Germany as Institutsleiter of Fraunhofer MEVIS and Professor of Medical Image Computing at the University of Bremen, while continuing his activities in the U.S. On March 1, 2020, he returned to Boston full time and was appointed the B. Leonard Holman Professor of Radiology, Harvard Medical School, and Vice-Chair for Biomedical Informatics Research, Department of Radiology, Brigham and Women’s Hospital.

  • Dr. Christos Davatzikos

    Dr. Christos Davatzikos is the Wallace T. Miller Sr. Professor of Radiology at the University of Pennsylvania, and Director of the Center for Biomedical Image Computing and Analytics. He holds a secondary appointment in Electrical and Systems Engineering at Penn as well as at the Bioengineering an Applied Mathematics graduate groups. He obtained his undergraduate degree by the National Technical University of Athens, Greece in 1989, and his Ph.D. degree from Johns Hopkins, in 1994, on a Fulbright scholarship. He then joined the faculty in Radiology and later in Computer Science, where he founded and directed the Neuroimaging Laboratory. In 2002 he moved to Penn, where he founded and directed the section of biomedical image analysis. Dr. Davatzikos’s interests are in medical image analysis. He oversees a diverse research program ranging from basic problems of imaging pattern analysis and machine learning, to a variety of clinical studies of aging and Alzheimer’s Disease, schizophrenia, brain cancer, and brain development. Dr. Davatzikos has served on a variety of scientific journal editorial boards and grant review committees. He is an IEEE fellow, and a fellow of the American Institute for Medical and Biological Engineering.

14h40-15h40 Dr. Ron Kikinis (Keynote) Harvard Medical School Navigation for the Rest of the World

Navigation technology has found uses in a variety of interventional procedures. This technology is being used to merge image-derived, subject-specific information with the patient's anatomy. Such merged information allows the physicians to see what they could not otherwise and to navigate tools and instruments to achieve better results. These technologies are not available in most low and middle income countries (LMIC) due to the costs of equipment and consumables, and the need for specialized training to use the technology. Recent developments have significantly lowered the cost of navigation equipment, including ultrasound imaging scanners, motion tracking systems and navigation software, but end-to-end solutions targeting specific procedures have yet to be developed. To be practical, such solutions need to be compliant with relevant laws and adjusted to boundary conditions in LMICs. Two examples will be discussed in greater detail in order to demonstrate the challenges and possible solutions.

Thursday Sep 9th Multiple Sclerosis
14h30-14h40 Pitches Multiple sclerosis
14h40-15h40 Dr. Daniel Reich (Keynote) National Institutes of Health Beyond the McDonald Criteria: Which Advanced Multiple Sclerosis Imaging Markers Should Make It to Your Clinic?

In the 1990s, following the discovery that it could noninvasively identify white matter lesions and track their development, MRI revolutionized the diagnosis of multiple sclerosis (MS) and the search for disease-modifying treatments. Four generations of MRI-based diagnostic rules, known as the McDonald Criteria, have ensued, but there remains a substantial problem of misdiagnosis and over/undertreatment. To improve diagnostic accuracy and better capture the radiological correlates of slowly worsening disability known as progression, and to ensure that imaging measures are optimally applied to individuals (rather than populations), at least 5 challenges need to be overcome: (1) distinguishing MS lesions from nonspecific white matter lesions; (2) finding new MS lesions in the setting of high lesion burden; (3) differentiating chronic lesion types and tracking their evolution over time; (4) identifying leptomeningeal inflammation and subpial cortical lesions; and (5) assessing brain atrophy in the clinical setting. In this talk, I will give a high-level overview and present specific examples to illustrate the approach our lab has taken to addressing these issues and applying them in the clinic.
